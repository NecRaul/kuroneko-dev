Renovations and its consequences

Although in my blogpost about monologues I said I would be writing about Nukitashi followed by Mushoku Tensei, I had an abundance of free time on my hands in-between reading two VNs. I already explained this was due to renovations, but I'd like to give more context. Simply put, my room's renovations took way longer than expected. I had already disassembled my PC, desk, and bed and moved them, along with all other furniture out of my room, when the manual laborers who worked on the rest of the house informed me they couldn't come by for a couple of days. This couple of days turned into a little longer than a week, and more problems ensued during the renovation process, which extended the time where I couldn't access my PC to read Nukitashi 2.

In this abundance of free time, having nothing else to do, I re-read Katawa Shoujo on my PS Vita, finished reading Real mo Tama ni wa Uso o Tsuku and 2.5-jigen no Yuuwaku, read Kagurabachi, Maria no Danzai and Cuprum no Hanayome, and watched a lot of movies after setting up a media stack.

Two things to mention: First, I won't be talking about Katawa Shoujo here, as it deserves its own blogpost, although I can't guarantee that it will get one. Second, most of these are my loosely connected, lightly edited notes that I wrote down on what I thought about these movies right after I had seen them. I put a bit more effort into the ones I really liked or had been looking forward to watching. I also tried to put more effort into the manga section, but admittedly, it's much harder to write about a manga I've read over the course of a couple of years than it is to jot down my thoughts about a movie I just watched.

Media Stack

I had decided on not going into much detail regarding the media stack, but I spent a considerable amount of time getting everything the way I wanted, so now I feel compelled to write about it.

The initial setup, using some official Arch Linux packages and some AUR packages of Jellyfin and Arr applications, was pretty easy and worked pretty well. I could download movies from my phone (or TV, but it would've been a pain to search for movies with a TV remote) and watch them on the Android TV Jellyfin client (or on my phone, but you get the idea). However, I didn't wanna dedicate a couple of gigabytes on my root partition to just a media stack, so I migrated everything over to Docker.

I haven't worked with Docker or other container services that much, since generally someone else set up everything at my job and I only had to run it. I have worked with YAML for CI/CD stuff before, so that part wasn't unfamiliar. The only annoyance was the lack of good documentation for the different images I was using.

I made a home directory for the Docker user and eventually set up everything perfectly. I liked this workflow with containers so much I even migrated my qBittorrent instance. I still prefer the QT client over the web client, but the difference between them is minimal, and I could use the web client effectively right away. The last thing I added to my media stack was Jellyseerr, which I'll use to get requests from my friends for any movies they might want to watch. Down the line, I want to build a dedicated NAS with redundancy just for this type of thing.

As for a dashboard, I was using Homer for a while, but eventually I found Glance, which is a more featureful and nicer-looking alternative. I even cloned the repo, made some PRs, and published my own Docker image with patches for personal use until they merge my PRs.

Kinography

Since I had so much time with no computer at my disposal, I watched a lot of movies in a short period of time and thought about what movies I really love. Along with this post, I've added a Kino section to my 3x3 page, and soon enough, a music section should follow, where I put my favorite albums.

I'm not a movie lover. I'm not even a movie "liker". Most movies I don't care for in general, but out of the new movies I watched, Trainspotting and Buffalo '66 instantly became some of my favorite movies of all time. Both of these movies have very different themes and styles, and I liked them for different reasons. They each made me feel very different things. I was laughing my ass off at Trainspotting and how it undermines everything that happens in a junkie's life, contrary to movies like Requiem for a Dream where everything is taken so seriously that some of the over the top scenes become comedic. I was enamored and fascinated by the tension and the beauty portrayed in Buffalo '66. I don't really care about the criticism people have for it or how it might be seen as harmful. It was a movie that was worth every second, and God willing, I'll watch it many more times in the future.

Bram Stoker's Dracula was a decent, very well shot movie, but it was more of a style over substance experience for me. I like the original Nosferatu and the original Dracula movies more than this. I would say this is better than Robert Eggers' Nosferatu though.

Streets of Fire and The Rocky Horror Picture Show were entertaining to me since I like movies similar to them, like Phantom of the Paradise where music is the driving force, but I don't know if I would recommend them to other people. The other reason I grouped them together is that Jim Steinman wrote some of the music for one, and Meatloaf appears and sings in the other.

Equilibrium had decent action set pieces, especially the very first and last ones, were pretty great, but the movie itself failed to impress me in terms of everything else. The story was laughable. The one thing aside from the action scenes I liked was the idea that everything would be fixed in a day with no drugs to suppress emotion and feelings due to human nature.

Porky's was pretty funny all the way through, and I think it might be a movie I go back to with friends from time to time. Some scenes made me pause the movie because my laughter was disrupting the experience of others who were watching the movie with me.

I rewatched the three classic Soviet slapstick comedy movies featuring Shurik, and they were just as entertaining as I remembered. Since I have a crush on Natalya Varley, I decided to watch Viy for the first time as well and was pleasantly surprised by it. Viy does a lot of things well, and just the last 10 to 15 minutes is one of the best sequences in Soviet cinema I've seen. Its ending is somewhat left to interpretation, and even ignoring that, this is one of those movies where each person will have a somewhat different interpretation of the explored themes. I myself might have a different interpretation of them in subsequent watches. Overall, it's a pretty good movie, definitely worth watching.

I rewatched The Wizard of Oz with my mom, and we both enjoyed it. Overall, a great movie with a straightforward and positive message one can and should take to heart.

I rewatched the first three Indiana Jones movies, and though I didn't really care for The Temple of Doom and The Last Crusade, Raiders of the Lost Ark is pretty good. I wish it had ended with them escaping Cairo, but that's my only criticism of the movie.

Falling Down is one of the worst movies I've ever seen, and I regret watching it. I'm almost sure it is supposed to be propaganda, but they failed miserably at it, and the film turned into a joke. I felt the same way about Kremen and The Major, and honestly, Kremen is even worse than Falling Down. The Major is slightly better, but still not a very good movie.

I rewatched the Sam Raimi Spider-Man trilogy, and it was as good as I remembered it. Both the first and the second movies are top tier, with the third one having a lot of good moments, albeit being a little rushed due to having multiple villains in it. The ending of the first movie was like that scene from Ratatouille, where I was taken back to my childhood with the gosh darn Nickelback playing as the credits are flowing. It's nice to go back to some movies to see that time has treated them very well, and I enjoy them as much as I did back in the day.

Although I haven't watched it recently, the same thing could be said about the first Transformers movie, just like with Spider-Man, a good song as the cherry on top, this time from Linkin Park. The scene that stood out to me the most in the second movie was Dr. Octopus killing the doctors who are trying to operate on him. Essentially, Sam Raimi, to me, is first and foremost a horror movie director, and that scene reminded me of Evil Dead so much. The third movie's standout scene, to nobody's surprise, is the Sandman sequence.

I love James Jonah Jameson so much. I had forgotten the one scene in the second movie where he changes his opinion of Spider-Man due to Dr. Octopus' rampage and kidnapping of Mary Jane, and how he does a 180 as soon as Spider-Man steals back his uniform. Some of the shots in the movie will live rent free in my head for a while, like Spider-Man swinging through the city towards Dr. Octopus, only for it to zoom out and then transition into the view from Dr. Octopus' glasses. Simply brilliant.

Just like how I think Sam Raimi is one of the best directors still alive, I feel the same about Willem Dafoe regarding actors. I cannot imagine the first movie being as good as it is without him in it. You can change the villains or even the main characters' actors and actresses, and it wouldn't be such a downgrade. I didn't mention this in the Streets of Fire section earlier, but he's great in that movie as well.

Aside from what I already wrote, the elevator scene in the second movie felt Evangelionesque to me. The justice and its purpose, and how it should be enacted, was very sincere, from the reason Peter became Spider-Man to Aunt Mary's speech about how revenge drives men to do unjust things, and how they shouldn't be glad to see her beloved husband's murderer being killed by a hero.

And don't even get me started on the character moments. All the things I've talked about are great, but the real meat and potatoes are the character moments. From the first talk between Mary Jane and Peter in the backyard, to the break-up on the bridge, to Gwen Stacy's sadness followed by the apology to Mary Jane based on the fact she was used by emo Peter, all of it is gold. Even the emo Peter stuff is great! I know this part of the blogpost feels more disjointed, but that's because I'm fanboying and can't express what I feel for these movies sufficiently enough.

Star Wars was kind of a stinker. I watched both the originals and the prequels, and although I liked some of the human characters and, at times, C-3PO, I just didn't care for the originals that much. Han Solo is my favorite Harrison Ford character.

Regarding the original trilogy, the first movie is a bit middle of the road, the second movie is kind of meh, and the third movie is in a weird place where it's simultaneously the best for the themes it has, the funniest of the three, and the worst for all the weird stuff with the excessive use of Ewoks in it. I might have enjoyed A New Hope more if it was a one-and-done experience.

When it comes to the prequels… I liked them, sort of. They are dumb and unnecessary in the grand scheme of things, like the two sequels to A New Hope, but I liked parts of them more than the original trilogy. All the action scenes were much better done, the music was infinitely better, and unlike the main theme of the originals, none of the new tracks in the prequels got on my nerves. This might be more of a me issue, but there are a lot of good movie themes that get overused within the movie they are in, and it just starts to become grating to me. The Good, The Bad and The Ugly theme itself is pretty good, for example, but it gets overused in the movie, and by the final confrontation, I am just done with it. I feel the same about the original trilogy's main theme.

Going back to the prequels, all three of them have really good choreography, with Revenge of the Sith's simultaneous duels between Yoda and Palpatine, and Obi-Wan and Anakin being my favorites. The biggest problem of the prequels, in my opinion, is that they are prequels and have to make the events happen in a way where it doesn't retcon the originals. Anakin has to live even though Obi-Wan would have mercy-killed him if the movie didn't demand that he live. Both trilogies are houses of contrivances, but the prequels' house is stacked much higher.

If I had to rate the movies in comparison to one another, I’d say Revenge of the Sith is the best, followed by Return of the Jedi, The Phantom Menace, A New Hope, The Empire Strikes Back, and finally Attack of the Clones. I was surprised by how many people like The Empire Strikes Back, as even though it is the movie where Darth Vader’s relation to Luke is revealed, most people were probably spoiled on that revelation long before watching the movie.

I don't understand the Jar-Jar hate, since the originals have just as annoying characters, like R2-D2 and Chewbacca. Overall, Star Wars is not for me, meaning it's just not very good. One last complaint: the movies are too long. No, I shan't elaborate on that.

Spaceballs is probably the least funny Mel Brooks movie I've seen. In his other movies, the humor comes from actual jokes, while Spaceballs is a wink-wink, nudge-nudge type comedy. My lack of enjoyment might have to do with me not liking the thing it parodies as well.

I watched the first two Ip Man movies and got halfway through the third one, but I got bored and didn't finish it. I didn't have high hopes for the fourth one after that, so I skipped it. The action is alright, but that's about it.

I went on a bit of a Robin Williams spree here. I rewatched some of his movies I'd already seen, discovered a few I hadn't seen before, and revisited others I had seen a very long time ago and had forgotten. I'll focus on the latter two.

I had never seen Mrs. Doubtfire, and it's a pretty funny movie. My only issue with it is Miranda being worse than a combination of Hitler, Lenin, Mao, Stalin, Pol Pot, and Leopold II.

I had seen Jumanji and Zathura: A Space Adventure as a kid on DVD, but I had long forgotten about both. I rewatched Jumanji but not Zathura, and I liked Jumanji. It has some very well-written parts, like Alan realizing he has become very similar to his father even though he spent the last 26 years in a jungle, and eventually reconciling with him when the game ends. I disliked having the realization scene undercut with a joke, but what can you do.

Next, I watched What Dreams May Come, and truthfully, I didn't really like it. I teared up at certain parts, but a lot of ideas were a miss for me. One of the bigger ones being Marie continuing to use the Asian lady's body. The scene works fine, but I believe God made us with a mind and a body. The outer shell may be just appearances, but I wouldn't want to change it to be any better, worse, or different than I am. Just like my mind and soul are essential parts of what make me who I am, my body is the temple in which these spiritual parts of me reside. I understand they use their proper bodies at the end of the movie, but using the same trick three times also kills the emotional impact.

Another complaint I have is that the movie is sort of ugly. I understand the oil painting motif, but it looks worse in motion. The aesthetic elements get better as the movie goes on, but not that much better. I picked up on two things. First, the story reminded me of Orpheus and Eurydice, and I kept comparing the two throughout the movie. You just can't beat a Greek tragedy that has stood the test of time. The other is the three motif that appears a lot in the movie, like the Garden of Earthly Delights style painting with three parts to it. Although I think the first one is definitely a thought-out and direct nod, I can't be sure about the second one.

The thing connecting these three movies together is kids being a driving force behind a lot of the story, and I have a soft spot for kids, so I liked that. This last one is less of a Robin Williams movie and more of a movie that Robin Williams appears in, but it felt appropriate to mention it in this section. Well, just like what I said about the last three movies, this movie also has a kid as a driving force behind a lot of the story. It's Night at the Museum. I had seen it before but had forgotten all about it. It ain't great, but it's not terrible either. I didn't like Ricky Gervais, but the rest of the cast did their jobs properly. The Zoolander duo is back together and all.

This is a nitpick, but after the second night, I presumed the museum director would have more of a problem with one of the cavemen being gone rather than the remaining ones being covered in foam. I am not sure if this is the case, but this movie felt like a direct-to-DVD or TV type of thing.

The Irishman sucked, much like other Martin Scorsese movies. I've seen Taxi Driver, Goodfellas, and The Wolf of Wall Street, and none of them are good. This is another one to add to the pile. I'll watch The Last Temptation of Christ followed by The Passion of the Christ (although this one isn't a Scorsese movie) sometime this year, and I hope the former will finally be the one good movie from his filmography. Aside from that, I have The Departed and Shutter Island on my watchlist as well, but I'll be done with Scorsese movies after that. Hopefully, he'll be done with life too.

Segueing into talking about The Irishman, I kinda didn't wanna see these geriatrics on my screen, especially de-aged. Many humorous moments stemmed from them being way too old to act like younger men.

I think this movie isn't as bad as Goodfellas, but that's more due to how, instead of a live fast, die young type of story, it's a story about worthless men whose power was only good for hurting those who lived more honest and fulfilling lives than them, only for them to end up with broken families, not being visited by anyone except the FBI, and having almost no remorse even at the end of their lives.

I can enjoy characters who were written to be bad, but in all the cases where I have, they were either very charismatic or had, albeit not justifiable, an understandable reason for their villainous acts. Izaya from Durarara!! and Fuminori from Saya no Uta come to mind. I don't remember the name, but there was a Japanese movie with yakuza as one of the subplots, and I didn't really like that portion of the movie either. So, it's not even Thing, Japan being better. It might be Kids Return but as I said, I don't remember it very well.

Speaking of geriatrics playing the Italian mob, I watched Ghost Dog: The Way of the Samurai. They should've called this movie Ghost Dogshit because it was not very good. I could go into detail about it, but simply put, samurai, just like many Japanese things, are cringe. A black guy larping as a samurai doesn't make it any better.

Training Day was kinda meh. I had heard it was an anxiety-inducing movie, but it didn't have that effect on me. I don't have much to say about it aside from that.

I felt the same for Good Time: it was alright, but nothing special. Uncut Gems by the same directors was a better movie with more or less the same story structure.

I watched some of the classic horror movies. Most of the movies I talked about in the blogpost, I talked about them in the order that I watched them in. However, I watched these horror movies here and there, taking a break from other movies.

Night of the Living Dead was shit. Having a semi-competent black guy in it doesn't a good movie make. I don't know whether it was revolutionary when it came out, nor do I care, as it was a bore for me.

Child's Play was alright, but nothing special. A friend told me it was actually scary, but I guess he must have seen it as a young teenager or a kid.

A Nightmare on Elm Street was amazing. It could've been one of my top 3 horror movies of all time if not for that awful ending. I had to watch this one while hearing my dad complain about the type of movies I watch, and due to having to explain what I liked so much about it, I really put effort into thinking about what makes it special. Aside from all the obvious stuff, I guess I have a soft spot for teenagers not getting taken seriously when they are right.

I started watching Scream, but 15 minutes into the movie the subtitle sync got messed up, so I switched to Friday the 13th. Funnily enough, during the first 15 minutes of Scream, Friday the 13th's ending was spoiled for me. I didn't really like Friday the 13th. I don't care for slasher movies that much in general, but this one was especially boring.

I then went back to watch Scream with a different subtitle track, which ended up still being broken, but I did finish the movie. I can't say I enjoyed it, however. Too predictable for my tastes, along with being too referential. I'm not a horror movie guy, so I'm not intimately familiar with the rules, cliches, and the tropes of the genre, but I figured out quite a bit of the ending early on. I think it was around Tatum's death I guessed the existence of multiple killers, and guessed that Billy was one of the killers when it became evident he had watched many horror movies and could have been at the scene of every crime.

Last but not least, although this isn't a movie, it felt appropriate to talk about it here. A couple of months back, I watched the first two seasons of Smiling Friends in one sitting and enjoyed them quite a bit. Then season 3 started to come out and I started watching new episodes of the show as they were coming out, and I also enjoyed them quite a bit. I felt as if this season had episodes that were funnier than those of previous seasons, while also having a couple of stinkers. High highs, low lows type of thing. I have seen all eight episodes, but Sonarr shows two more episodes having been produced. I don't know much about that, but yeah, Smiling Friends was decently funny.

There are many other movies I watched but omitted from the blogpost, since most of them either didn't leave much of an impression on me or weren't my cup of tea to begin with. There are also a couple of movies that I omitted, even though I liked them a lot, because if I were to talk about them, I would need a whole new blogpost.

Manga

Real mo Tama ni wa Uso o Tsuku is a very short manga. Each chapter is 2 to 6 pages long. You can read it in its entirety in a couple of hours. I had been reading it for a couple of years and had gotten quite bored of it. Although the chapters themselves are very short, the wait between them and their scanlation is not. I had actually stopped keeping up with it for a couple of months and only learned of its ending from an acquaintance's post on a media thread. Needless to say, I read the rest of the chapters in about 15 minutes and felt the ending was rushed, especially compared to the arc that preceded it, which felt dragged out to me.

2.5 Jigen no Yuuwaku is a manga I started because it had cute girls in cosplay. It was an ecchi manga where softcore nudity was present. I didn't take it seriously for a long time because it wasn't very serious. I didn't really care for the character of Lilysa because she didn't have many problems. The majority of the story went ahead with her having no arc of her own until the last quarter of the story, where her arc ended up being a misunderstanding of sorts. I disliked that a lot. She was also a big Mary Sue. Everyone quickly became friends with her, even supposed rivals came to like her a lot, to the point of fighting for her attention.

I thought the male main character, Okumura, wasn't much better. He was like a worse version of Keima from TWGOK. In a way, everything in this manga could be summarized as an inferior version of TWGOK. However, it has its saving graces. In regards to TWGOK, it had been pretty obvious for a long time that he would end up with a girl because he had moved past his baggage. The same wasn't true for Okumura. There had been bits and pieces of him finally loving 3D, but loving 3D and loving a person are worlds apart. There were definitely hints as to who he liked too, but I would still argue it wasn't very concrete. Although I have spoken rather negatively about this manga, I did like it, and at the end, the first thought that came to my mind was that this was Evangelion 4.0, done much better.

Speaking of Evangelion 4.0, Fate/Grand Order ended. lol. lmao, even. Get fucked gachafags.

Kagurabachi is not very good. I read the first three or so chapters when that was all that existed and thought maybe I would go back to it later. Eventually, when it reached 100 chapters, I caught up. It has some dumb story moments, but my main problem is how the story arbitrarily puts characters out of commission for very long stretches. This happens to everyone, including the main character. Some characters who I thought would be very important to the story have not been seen in more than 70 chapters.

The art has also regressed a lot compared to the early chapters. Some background characters are basically sketches at this point. That said, I do not understand why people treat this as a black sheep. It is not as if other manga currently published in Shonen Jump are any better or worse than this. In fact, most manga published in the same magazine in the past had similar quality, except for the art, and the decline in art quality in Kagurabachi is fairly recent and hopefully only a temporary issue.

I think a lot of people fell for the meme that it was good when it first started and then became jilted when it became clear that it was the same as many others that came before it.

Maria no Danzai is in the same vein as Shounen no Abyss, but at least that manga had some entertaining moments, while this one is just boring. It also flip flops about being a revenge story. The mom is kind of hot, and I think I even got into it because of a mom thread on /a/.

It must have been four or five years ago. I was going through a period where I didn’t know what I wanted to do with my life. Every day felt the same. Now, I take that as a blessing, but back then it felt as if my life had ended. I can’t even pin down why I felt that way at the time. Maybe I could see on the horizon an end to my peaceful days with friends from university. Maybe it was the anxiety of entering the real world and having to look for a job. Maybe it was something very inconsequential. Regardless, I was at a turbulent point in my life.

One of these days, someone asked me for a favor. They had a personal matter to attend to but couldn’t close their shop for a whole day. I didn’t really want to do it, but I didn’t have it in me to say no. So, I opened up early in the morning and started reading Ookami Shounen wa Kyou mo Uso wo Kasaneru. I still had to attend to customers, so it took me the whole day to finish it. I remember feeling a strange mix of giddiness and sadness as I neared the end. I didn’t want it to conclude in a way I didn’t like. Thankfully, it didn’t. It ended perfectly and became my favorite romance manga.

Not much has changed since then, apparently, because I had a pretty similar experience reading the first six volumes (that’s all the translated English volumes at the moment) of Cuprum no Hanayome. I can’t even tell how much of this is me genuinely loving the manga and how much of it is me just being happy to read another namo work. Regardless of how much that influences my opinion, Cuprum no Hanayome is a pretty good manga. It’s lighthearted, cute, funny, and even bittersweet at times. The idea of your beloved person influencing your creative work isn’t new, but I had never seen it applied to this type of craft before. This one, along with Ookami Shounen wa Kyou mo Uso wo Kasaneru, comes highly recommended from me.

Closing notes

This is certainly the longest blogpost I’ve ever written, and I don’t think I’ll ever top it in terms of length. I had a lot to say about movies, and I wanted to get it all out of my system. I made a Letterboxd account and a Serializd account to go along with my MyAnimeList account and spreadsheets. I have also edited my about page to reflect these new additions to my media consumption habits.

At the end of it all, I guess I have a few things to say about movies and TV shows. I don’t think I’ll ever watch a TV show with 40 to 45 minute episodes again. When I look back at the ones I finished, it feels exhausting. They aren’t as good as movies tend to be, but they take an extreme amount of time compared to almost anything else.

Even the longest visual novels usually top out around 120 to 140 hours, but some of these TV shows go on forever. Not only do I get disinterested, they never manage to keep the same level of quality, and I’m talking about the good ones here.

If I'm being honest, most of TV shows I watched were something I got pulled into by a former girlfriend or a girl I wanted to be my girlfriend. So what I'm trying to say is that TV shows feel like worse versions of movies made by people who aren't trusted with making actual movies. And I already prefer other mediums over movies to begin with.

Aside from all that, I attended the yearly Sora no Woto rewatch stream with /a/. It had been a couple of years since I had the time to watch it, join any watch-alongs with /a/, hang out for post-stream r/a/dio, or chat with people in the r/a/dio IRC. Suffice it to say, it was as good as I remembered it, nay, even better, and maybe I only enjoyed it that much because of the whole experience, but what the hell. Due to how much I enjoyed it, I also updated my Anime 3x3, replacing Denpa Onna to Seishun Otoko with Sora no Woto. The former is great in its own right, but there is something about Sora no Woto that I cannot quite put into words. I guess this will be my other recommendation: it is great, and you should give it a watch.

The next post, which will be about Mushoku Tensei, is long overdue. I was planning to finish Nukitashi 2 by the end of November at the latest and write the blogpost a couple of days after that, but things outside of my control kept happening, so I ended up working on this one alongside it.

The Mushoku Tensei blogpost should be shorter than this one or the Nukitashi blogpost. I already have most of my thoughts in order, but I might wait until Lucius Seneca finishes his translation of Paul Gaiden and I re-read it before I start writing that blogpost.
